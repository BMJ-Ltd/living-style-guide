Tone of voice

Introduction

What is a tone of voice?

A tone of voice is the personality of a brand. It works alongside a company’s visual identity. It also helps to define a company and bring its brand values to life. BMJ’s tone of voice needs to be consistent to be recognisable.

Why is tone of voice guidance important?

Guidance helps to inform all of a company’s written copy. By following the principles in this guide, you can make your content more accessible, succinct and direct for your reader.

Why do we need one?

It’s an expression of who we are

As a company, we have worked hard to establish what we do. Who we are is embodied by our brand personality and values, and drive how we interact with the medical community.

It builds trust

Our reputation is built on trust, credibility and transparency. How we communicate enforces this trust.

It connects us better with our readers

We know a lot about our customers: who they are and what their needs are.They don’t have time to shift through the plethora of information they currently receive. By following the principles in this guide, you can make your content more accessible, succinct and direct for your audience.

Our tone of voice defined

Our brand’s four most powerful personality traits have steered the thinking behind the company’s tone of voice.

Confident

We believe in ourselves as pioneering experts within our profession. Evidence matters to us so, wherever possible, we use data to back-up our claims

Empowering

We motivate, support and enable healthcare professionals in all communities worldwide—so the words we choose to use are clear and accessible for everyone.

Succinct

We know our users and customers are busy so we speak briefly, directly and clearly. We tell them what they need to know within the first two sentences.

Trustworthy

We are honest, authentic and credible. We know who we are writing for and meet our audience’s specific needs with thoughtful, relevant information that is correct in every detail.

Our words will best engage people when they stimulate both an emotional and an intellectual response. For us, emotional language alone would be too flighty, undermining our trustworthy credibility. Using only intellectual language would be too dry and potentially pompous – alienating audiences such as medical students and clinical managers.

Write powerfully

Basics for better writing

Write first, refine later. It is easiest if you get down what you want to say, then fine-tune it.

1. Be specific and use facts

Don’t use ‘corporate speak’, clichés and generic terms. Facts, figures and quotes will ground your statements in reality.

Accredit sources to give your writing more credibility. To be credible, customer testimonials should not be more than 3 years old.

Instead of saying:

BMJ OnExamination users will have a better chance of passing their exams.

Say:

With more than 60,000 students taking their MRCP exams each year, we make it our business to prime students to get the best possible results.

2. Be direct, use you

Talk directly to your reader in the active voice. Saying ‘you will/your users’ not ‘users will’ helps readers connect with your words and be active participants.

When talking or writing as the voice of the company, always refer to BMJ as we, us or our.

Instead of saying:

BMJ Examination users will be stretched and have their knowledge put to the test.

Say:

BMJ OnExamination will stretch you and put your knowledge to the test.

3. It’s not all about us

Don’t just talk about how great we are and what we can give users, customers and the healthcare community. Include them in the conversation too – emphasise ‘we’ and ‘you’. Sound like a trusted partner on the same level.

Instead of saying:

Showcasing the latest ideas and leading speakers, the conference aims to bring knowledge and solutions to support the future of medical and healthcare leadership and management.”

Say:

One of the key themes of the conference is learning from other industries. It’s here you will learn from other people who have achieved great success in their own industry. Come to find out how you can get more from your daily work life.

4. Remove the doubt

Avoid words like ‘could’, ‘should’, ‘would’, ‘might’, ’committed to’ and ‘aim to’.

Instead of saying:

The conference aims to bring knowledge and solutions...

Say:

The conference will help you understand what you need to know...

5. Use verbs for momentum

Focus on what a reader can do or learn and use verbs to illustrate this. ‘Understand’ is more powerful than ‘gain knowledge’, for example.

Instead of saying:

We deliver the highest standard of medical information and resources to improve outcomes for patients and the provision of healthcare worldwide.

Say:

We are helping clinicians to improve patient care by supplying accurate and innovative medical support, educational tools and events.

6. Use stories

Find examples of excellence you can draw on. Bring your copy to life with real stories and impressive feats that illustrate our success. We have a bank of these here if you need somewhere to start.

Showing is more powerful than telling.

For example: “Winning the Prevention Team of the Year award has inspired us to develop even further. Receiving the national recognition that the award brings has aided us in progressing the service.” Kerry Lyons, Hospital Alcohol Liaison team leader, Tameside Hospital NHS Foundation Trust, Greater Manchester.

7. Cut it back

Keep your writing simple, concise and clear. Too many words will bury the important messages.

Instead of saying:

Quality Improvement Reports is an open access, PubMed-indexed repository of global quality improvement. Share your work to inspire others and contribute to the international quality movement, or find innovative new approaches from peers around the world who are facing the same challenges as you.

Say:

Many of you make considerable contributions to healthcare. Would you like to see your efforts recognised by peers internationally? BMJ Quality Improvement Reports gives you the tools to successfully submit your work for publication in an open access, PubMed-indexed journal.

8. Be clear: avoid jargon

Avoid academic or marketing speak; complex ideas can still be expressed clearly and simply without losing their credibility.

Keep your flow of ideas clear. Present no more than one or two ideas in each paragraph.

Spell acronyms out in full first with their acronym following in brackets: Continuing Professional Development (CPD). Thereafter, the acronym may be used.

Instead of saying:

As one of the world’s leading STM publishers and a globally trusted and recognised brand, BMJ can provide your publication with the bespoke service it deserves. BMJ places the needs of our partners at the centre of all we do, and as a society ourselves we share the same values as our society partners.

Say:

We are one of the world’s leading Science, Technical, Medical (STM) publishers. Partner with us today so we can provide your publication with the bespoke service it deserves.

9. Write as you speak

Don’t be afraid to write like you speak. It will make you come across as more welcoming, accessible and collaborative.

Read your finished piece out loud.

Instead of saying:

At BMJ, our Learning and Development programme is focused on inspiring staff about fulfilling their career aspirations

Say:

know you want to progress your career. Our Learning and Development programme gives you the practical help to have a long-term career at BMJ.

10. Ask questions

Draw your reader into the conversation and invite action.

Instead of saying: Evidence-Based Nursing (EBN) is designed to alert practising nurses to important and clinically relevant advances in treatment, diagnosis, aetiology, and prognosis.

Say: Are you inspired by the latest developments in the field of nursing? Curious to know how studies impact on clinical practice?

11. Call to action

We often send communications in the hope that they’ll prompt the reader to do something. Empower your reader by including a call to action (CTA) that is specific, direct and actionable. If you have more than one CTA, focus attention on your primary goal.

Instead of saying:

How do I find out more? Read our success stories and fact sheets. Contact a member of our institutional sales team. You can stay up to date with BMJ by following us Twitter, Google+ and Facebook or joining our LinkedIn group.

Say:

How do I find out more? Contact our sales team for more information. Or, read our success stories and fact sheets. You can also find us on Twitter, Facebook, Google+ and LinkedIn.

12. Avoid medical clichés

The following claims appear again and again on healthcare company websites the world over. Try to avoid using them if possible – or at least use them sparingly. They won’t make us distinctive or memorable in the healthcare marketplace – we’ll just sound like everyone else.

Instead of saying:

  • World-class
  • Excellence
  • Unique
  • High-quality
  • High-standard
  • Cutting-edge
  • Solutions

Say:

We all have conventions for using: ‘high quality’; ‘comprehensive’ and ‘online resources.’ Try mixing these up with more accessible words, such as ‘highest ranked’, ‘most cited’, ‘accessible’, ‘useful’, ‘thorough’, ‘detailed’, ‘influential’ and so on.

All in the detail

Nothing says professional like perfectly polished copy. An errant apostrophe, misspelling or rogue typo has the capacity to undermine your message entirely. Cast a keen eye over everything you send out, even if it’s just an email.

Writing original and memorable copy

You will retain a reader’s attention by making your writing original and memorable by borrowing poetic language forms that are easy to apply such as alliteration, assonance and repetition. This works particularly well in advertising.

Alliteration

Repetition of the same starting sound in several words in a sentence. For example: create consistent content

Assonance

Similarity of sounds in words or syllables. For example: choose and use

Repetition

If you repeat on purpose, repetition is a clever writing tool. It’s acceptable to use a word or phrase around 2-4 times – any more than that and it starts to become pretty obvious.

 

Succinct

You know your users and customers are busy so always speak briefly, directly and clearly. Aim to tell them what they need to know within the first two sentences. Use short sentences and insert one or two longer sentences to add pace. And don’t be afraid to start sentences with ‘And’ and ‘But’ for emphasis.

For example: We have a vision to create a healthier world. And it’s working.

Look to the future

Use the present and future tense to connect with young audiences and the pioneers of tomorrow. If you’re talking about our heritage and past achievements, do it in the context of what we’re doing today and are set to achieve tomorrow.

Instead of saying:

BMJ has been publishing high quality innovative research since 1840.

Say:

Since starting out as the publisher of a single medical journal in 1840, we have evolved into a global healthcare knowledge provider.

 

Empowering

We’re here to support and enable, so our words need to be clear and accessible. Motivate readers with mini stories about the difference our products and services make – to careers, to day-to-day practice, to global health. Include a call to action.

Instead of saying:

The PACK guide is a comprehensive, integrated approach to adult primary care that includes 40 common symptoms and 20 chronic conditions. It aims to guide the clinician to assess a patient’s symptoms, make a diagnosis and devise a management plan, and deliver routine care for one or more chronic conditions. The content is informed by evidence sourced through the BMJ’s Best Practice, WHO guidelines and other sentinel guidelines, and is updated on an annual basis. It is designed to be practical and easy to use, with algorithms, checklists, illustrations and colour-coding.

Say:

The Practical Approach to Care Kit is currently guiding clinicians and nurses working in Brazil and Africa through patient assessments. Covering 40 common symptoms and 20 chronic conditions, the evidence-based content is sourced primarily through BMJ Best Practice and WHO guidelines. Its practical and easy to use design is proving to be an invaluable tool for over 6,000 clinics across South Africa.*

Be dynamic

We’re high achievers and doers, so use the active not passive voice. Use the imperative form of verbs (‘ask us’, ‘find out more’) to empower readers to inspire action and be decisive.

 

Trustworthy

Present evidence to back up the claims

Include facts, quotes and examples into your writing to pack a punch and ground it in real achievement. Show how trustworthy we are with confident language and celebrate successes with the prominence they deserve. Don’t bury impressive facts – give them emphasis as stand-out statistics, pull out quotes and email subject lines.

Instead of saying:

A recent independent review of clinical decision support tools published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research ranked BMJ Best Practice equal first for breadth of disease coverage, editorial quality, and evidence-based methodology.

Say:

BMJ Best Practice ranked equal first for breadth of disease coverage, editorial quality, and evidence-based methodology. The independent review of clinical decision support tools is published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research.

Be accessible to your audiences

Prospective subscribers fresh from their medical exams will not be attracted to our products by a tone of writing used for society proposals. Similarly, when addressing institutional customers, your tone of writing will be different from the one you use when talking to colleagues on Watercooler+. Our tone of voice needs enough flex to connect with different audiences, but enough consistency to remain distinctly BMJ.

A-levels and medical students

Talk to students as partners informally, using language and turns of phrase that are familiar to them. But don’t try too hard. Bump up the confident and trustworthy, backed up with success stories that will inspire them.

External academics and researchers

Don’t patronise the reader with language that is stuffy and convoluted. Focus on ‘confident’ by being clear and making your point upfront. Make stand out with the ‘empowering’ with a call to action.

Clinicians

Show real-world relevance by communicating confident and trustworthy’ in a way that catches the eye of busy business people. Combine creativity using the poetic language forms to make your copy memorable.

Communities on social media.

Use collaborative language – ‘we’, ‘our’ and ‘you’ – to invite people to join the conversation. Use ‘empowering’ language to break down communication barriers and involve the wider community.

Structure

Readers tend to scan pages How you structure your writing will determine whether anyone will read it.

1. Be upfront

Structure your content so that the most relevant facts and statements are in the first paragraph. It’ll draw your reader in and compel them to read on. Focus on the clear flow of ideas to make your words count. Introductions should be about 20–30 words, summarising the page content and highlighting key themes or most interesting facts.

Specifically for online content, the opening paragraph of every web page is a summary of the page. This helps visitors gain a quick understanding of the page content and its relevance to them.

Keep your headlines around 6 words or less. Try to keep your main body of content around 300–400 words or less: if you have more to say, break it up into easy-to-navigate chunks.


2. Use actionable subject lines for emails and use informative subheadings

Subject lines that begin with action verbs will entice your reader. Make your emails more clickable by adding a vibrant verb at the beginning.

Within the email or body copy of a print advert or webpage, subheadings break your text into manageable chunks. This will catch your readers’ attention and make the page easier to navigate or scan.

Specifically for web content. Structuring your writing this way will advertise your content to both visitors and search engines.


3. Accentuate key points

Use bullet points, tables, graphics or boxes to gather important information in one place. Start bullet points with a verb if possible, to inspire action and empower the readers.


4. Call to action

Does your content have a purpose? A call to action (CTA) asks visitors to do something. What is your call to action? No content should be without a call to action.
